# Career Story and Role Fit

This file explains why Barbara chose cybersecurity and systems-focused work, why she is capable of doing it, and how her academic work, projects, and training connect to her target career path.

## Why I Chose Cybersecurity and Systems Work

I chose cybersecurity because I naturally think in systems. I like understanding how different parts of technology connect, how data moves, where access is controlled, where risk enters, and how problems affect the whole environment.

Cybersecurity is not only about stopping attacks. It is about protecting flow, stability, trust, and control across people, devices, networks, applications, cloud systems, and data.

My interest grew through hands-on learning in networking, Linux, cloud computing, programming, infrastructure security, ethical hacking, penetration testing, incident response, and cybersecurity architecture. Each course helped me see more of the full system and how security controls work together.

## Why I Can Do This Work

I can do this work because I have built a strong academic and hands-on foundation in cybersecurity, networking, cloud systems, operating systems, programming, and technical documentation.

My academic background shows consistency, discipline, and follow-through. I have maintained a 4.00 GPA while completing cybersecurity and networking coursework, earning an Associate of Applied Science in Cybersecurity and Networking, and completing undergraduate certificates in Cyber Security and Information Technology Essentials with honors.

I have also completed projects and labs involving IoT systems, Linux administration, Python/API data analysis, networking, cloud computing, infrastructure security, penetration testing, incident response, and security architecture.

## What Makes Me Different

I do not look at technology as isolated parts. I look at the whole environment:

- What assets exist?
- How are they connected?
- Where does data flow?
- Who has access?
- What controls are missing?
- What symptoms show a problem?
- What needs to be documented?
- What fix restores stability?

This systems view helps me understand security as a living environment where every part affects the whole.

## Why I Fit Cloud Security Roles

I fit cloud security roles because my coursework and projects connect cloud computing, infrastructure security, access control, logging, monitoring, data protection, and risk documentation.

Cloud security requires understanding how systems are designed, configured, monitored, and controlled. That matches how I already think about cybersecurity.

## Why I Fit API Security and Vulnerability Work

I fit API security and vulnerability work because I am interested in how systems communicate and where data exposure can happen.

APIs are connection points between systems. If authentication, authorization, rate limiting, validation, or logging are weak, the whole system can become exposed.

My API security learning focuses on responsible testing, authorized environments, information disclosure, access control, and documenting findings clearly.

## Why I Fit AI and Cybersecurity Work

I fit AI and cybersecurity work because I understand that AI systems need boundaries, human review, limitation statements, risk awareness, and responsible design.

My ClarityFlow AI project helped me practice building an AI-supported decision tool while considering harm, bias, user safety, and human oversight.

My long-term interest is in secure AI-enabled systems, cloud security, and cybersecurity workflows where AI supports decision-making without removing human judgment.

## My Professional Promise

I am building toward a career where I help organizations make their systems more secure, visible, reliable, and controlled.

My goal is to bring together cybersecurity, cloud systems, API security, Linux troubleshooting, responsible AI, documentation, and systems thinking to support secure infrastructure and reduce risk.

## Hands-On Problem Solving Outside of School

My interest in systems does not only come from school. In my spare time, I work on practical systems such as cars, houses, repairs, and technical projects.

Fixing cars and working on houses taught me how to diagnose symptoms, understand connected parts, test safely, plan repairs, and restore function. That same mindset applies to cybersecurity and systems engineering.

Whether the system is a vehicle, a house, a network, a cloud environment, or an API, I approach the problem by asking what is connected, where the flow is breaking, what control is missing, and what fix will restore stability.

This is why security-focused systems work fits me. It matches how I already solve problems in real life.
